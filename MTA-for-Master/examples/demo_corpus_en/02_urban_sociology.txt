Urban sociology analyzes the transformation of cities and the ways of life associated with them. The Chicago School, in the nineteen twenties, observes spatial segregation, transition zones and urban ecology. Robert Park and Louis Wirth describe the city as a specific way of life, marked by anonymity, density and the heterogeneity of populations. Later, Henri Lefebvre theorizes the right to the city and criticizes functionalist urbanism that separates residential, commercial and industrial functions. Gentrification, urban sprawl and socio-spatial inequalities become major objects of inquiry. Relations between housing, social classes and public policies structure contemporary debates on urban production. Recent research also integrates ecological issues, mobility, and the demographic transformations of metropolitan areas.
