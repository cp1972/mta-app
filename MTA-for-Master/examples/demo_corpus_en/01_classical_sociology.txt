Classical sociology studies social structures and class relations in industrial societies. Emile Durkheim develops the notion of organic solidarity to describe modern societies, in contrast with the mechanical solidarity of traditional societies. This distinction shapes a large part of French sociology throughout the twentieth century. Max Weber, for his part, proposes a comprehensive sociology that focuses on the meaning actors give to their actions. Relations of domination, legal-rational authority, charisma and tradition become central categories for analyzing power in societies. Bureaucracy, as a modern form of organization, illustrates this process of rationalization. Pierre Bourdieu later extends these analyses by showing how social structures reproduce themselves through schools and cultural institutions, by way of the habitus and cultural capital of the dominant classes.
