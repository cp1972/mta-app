Political economy studies the production, distribution and consumption of wealth in societies. Adam Smith defends the division of labour and the regulating role of markets through the metaphor of the invisible hand. David Ricardo develops the theory of comparative advantage to analyze international trade. Karl Marx, in a radical critique, theorizes the exploitation of labour by capital, accumulation, and the periodic crises of the capitalist system. John Maynard Keynes proposes in the twentieth century a macroeconomic approach that justifies state intervention to support aggregate demand and limit unemployment. The monetary policies of central banks, interest rates and the money supply become essential levers. Contemporary debates still oppose neoclassical, Keynesian and heterodox approaches on the regulation of markets, inflation and economic growth.
